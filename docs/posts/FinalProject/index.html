Introduction

Over the past decade, basketball has been going through a shift, largely due to analytics. Teams have been viewing the three point shot as more and more valuable, while the mid-range slowly becomes a lost art. Throughout basketball history, people have always been encouraged to take a step in if they have a better chance of making the shot. However, as of late the game has shifted into the idea of getting as many threes and layups as possible.

Public opinion seems to say that this change has mainly effected the NBA, while college basketball sticks to traditional, fundamental game plans. People seem to think college teams, while obviously shooting more threes than they used to, haven’t fully subscribed to this new ideology of abandoning the mid range.

For this project, I’m using play-by-play data from the 2024-2025 NBA and NCAA seasons(found in the hoopR R package) to examine shot selection and efficiency at both levels of basketball. I want to determine if this evolution of the three pointer has really effected the NBA more than the college level, and also if this shift in ideology is even the right approach to begin with. Should teams continue to try and maximize their efficiency by eliminating mid range shots, or are teams that subscribe to this philosophy hurting themselves?

To tackle these questions, I’m going to be breaking the court into 12 hotzones, 5 zones behind the three point line, 5 zones in the midrange, a zone for shots at the rim, and a zone for other shots in the paint. I’m going to be looking at all 30 NBA teams from the 2025 season, but I’m filtering out some college teams so we are only looking at the top 100 teams from the 2025 season. That way we can filter out any weird outliers or really bad college teams.

Are teams shooting more threes?

To begin exploring how shot distribution has evolved, let’s first find out if this three point revolution is as drastic as people say.

library(tidyverse)
library(hoopR)
library(ggplot2)
# Load 10 years of Team Box Scores - because it's smaller than pbp data 
seasons <- 2016:2025

nba_box <- hoopR::load_nba_team_box(seasons = seasons)
ncaa_box <- hoopR::load_mbb_team_box(seasons = seasons)


# Calculate NBA Trends
nba_trend <- nba_box |>
  filter(field_goals_attempted > 0) |> 
  mutate(three_p_ar = three_point_field_goals_attempted / field_goals_attempted) |>
  group_by(season) |>
  summarize(three_rate = mean(three_p_ar, na.rm = TRUE), league = "NBA")

# Calculate NCAA Trends
ncaa_trend <- ncaa_box |>
  filter(field_goals_attempted > 0) |>
  mutate(three_p_ar = three_point_field_goals_attempted / field_goals_attempted) |>
  group_by(season) |>
  summarize(three_rate = mean(three_p_ar, na.rm = TRUE), league = "NCAA")



# Make the Plot
bind_rows(nba_trend, ncaa_trend) |>
  ggplot(aes(x = season, y = three_rate, color = league, group = league)) +
  geom_line(linewidth = 1.2) +
  theme_minimal() +
  scale_y_continuous(labels = scales::percent) +
  labs(
    title = "Are teams Shooting More threes?",
    y = "3-Point Attempt Rate (% of Total Shots)",
    x = "Season",
    color = "League"
  ) +
  scale_color_viridis_d() +
  scale_x_continuous(breaks = seq(2016, 2025, by = 2)) +
  theme_minimal()

This plot shows how team’s three point attempt rate has changed over the last decade for both NBA and NCAA teams. There is a clear increase in three point rate for both leagues, however the increase for the NBA is more drastic. Currently, the NBA rate is around 44%, while college is around 39%. It appears both could still be on the rise, although I would imagine there is a plateau coming soon.

I also found it interesting how the NCAA three point rate a decade ago was much higher than the NBA’s. This wasn’t what I was expecting because public perception has always been the NBA shoots more threes, and college plays more in the post. Despite the starting rate, the plot supports the idea that this three point revolution has effected the NBA much more drastically.

nba_raw <- hoopR::load_nba_pbp(seasons = 2025)

# filter for only shooting plays

                                 !str_detect(tolower(type_text), "freethrow"))

# clean nba data
nba_clean <- nba_shots |>
  # Filter for the half-court
  filter(abs(coordinate_x) <= 47 & abs(coordinate_y) <= 25) |>
  filter(!str_detect(home_team_name, "Team")) |> # filters out all star teams
  mutate(
    league = "NBA",
    shot_outcome = scoring_play,
    team_name = if_else(team_id == home_team_id, home_team_name, away_team_name),
    pts_value = if_else(str_detect(tolower(text), "three point"), 3, 2),
    
    # Distance from the hoop at (41.75, 0)
    shot_dist = sqrt((abs(coordinate_x) - 41.75)^2 + coordinate_y^2),
    # Depth from baseline 
    court_depth = 47 - abs(coordinate_x),
    # Width from center
    court_width = coordinate_y,
    
    
    # make the court zones
    shot_zone = case_when(
      shot_dist <= 4 ~ "Rim",
      shot_dist > 4 & shot_dist <= 14 & abs(court_width) <= 8 ~ "Paint",
      
      # 3-Pointers
      pts_value == 3 & court_depth <= 14 & court_width < 0 ~ "Left Corner 3",
      pts_value == 3 & court_depth <= 14 & court_width >= 0 ~ "Right Corner 3",
      pts_value == 3 & abs(court_width) <= 8 ~ "Top of the Key 3",
      pts_value == 3 & court_width < -8 ~ "Left Wing 3",
      pts_value == 3 & court_width > 8 ~ "Right Wing 3",
      
      # Mid-Range

      TRUE ~ "Other"

  ) |>

Shot Distribution

With three point attempts clearly on the rise, lets focus on just the 2025 year. First we can look at where exactly the most shots are being taken on the court.

# Compute shot count per zone per league
zone_counts <- shot_df |>
  count(league, shot_zone) |>
  group_by(league) |>
  mutate(shot_pct = n / sum(n) * 100)  # % of shots from that zone

# Join back so every point knows its zone's shot %
shot_df <- shot_df |>
  left_join(zone_counts, by = c("league", "shot_zone"))

# Plot color by shot_pct, increase point size to fill zones visually
ggplot(shot_df, aes(x = coordinate_y, y = 47 - abs(coordinate_x), color = shot_pct)) +
  geom_point(size = 1.1, alpha = 0.8) +
  facet_wrap(~league) +
  scale_color_gradient(
    name = "% of Shots",
    low = "#fde8e8",
    high = "#8b0000"
  ) + 
  coord_fixed() +
  theme_minimal() +
  labs(
    title = "Shot Distribution by Zone",
    x = NULL, y = NULL
  )

This displays shot distribution for each league. Each individual point is a shot attempt. Points are grouped by which of the 12 zones they are in, and colored by the % of shots are taken from that zone. Darker points mean more shots are taken from there.

It looks like both leagues follow generally the same pattern. The zones with the most shots are at the rim and in the paint, followed by threes. It’s clear that in both the NBA and NCAA, teams are moving away from the mid range shot. However, there are some subtle differences we can observe. The NBA seems to be a little darker from three, especially in the corners. NCAA looks a little darker in the paint, further supporting the idea that the NBA is embracing this revolution slightly more, while college teams might be a little slower to catch on.

Efficiency

To determine whether or not this is the correct approach, I created a new variable called points per shot. It is the mean of the outcome of a the shot times the value of the shot. So essentially, the average points generated per attempt.

zone_pts <- shot_df |>
  group_by(league, shot_zone) |>
  summarise(pts_per_shot = mean(shot_outcome * pts_value, na.rm = TRUE), .groups = "drop")

shot_df <- shot_df |>
  left_join(zone_pts, by = c("league", "shot_zone"))

ggplot(shot_df, aes(x = coordinate_y, y = 47 - abs(coordinate_x), color = pts_per_shot)) +
  geom_point(size = 1.1, alpha = 0.8) +
  facet_wrap(~league) +
  scale_color_gradient(
    name = "Pts/Shot",
    low = "#fde8e8",
    high = "#8b0000"
  ) +
  coord_fixed() +
  theme_minimal() +
  labs(
    title = "Points per Shot by Zone",
    x = NULL, y = NULL
  )

When looking at points per shot across the 12 zones, the picture changes dramatically. Shots at the rim are obviously the most valuable for both leagues, simply because they are so close that field goal percentages are much higher. After that, for both leagues the three point shot is the most valuable. Even threes that aren’t from the corner are beating out not only mid range shots, but also shots in the paint. For both leagues, mid range shots are clearly the worst value shot on the court. This is even more of the case for college teams, which much like the last plot depicting field goal percentages, showcases that college teams should be fully embracing this new philosophy of getting up as many threes as possible.

Conclusion

For this project, I wanted to examine how shot selection has evolved for both the NBA and NCAA. From this data and these visualizations, we can conclude that both levels are adapting to the idea of taking more threes. The NBA has been much more eager to increase their three point attempts, while the NCAA follows at a slightly slower pace. We can also safely say that this is the correct approach. Three point shots in the modern era are much more valuable than mid range shots and both leagues, but especially the NCAA should continue to utilize this.
